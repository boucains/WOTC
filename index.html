RE: Work Opportunity Tax Credit Value Proposition

Dear (Person),

The Work Opportunity Tax Credit (WOTC) is a federal program designed to make it more cost-effective to hire people in various categories who have faced significant barriers to employment. A tax credit is generated, up to a maximum amount, from a percent of wages paid to the eligible employee. The credit can be as high as $9,600. Currently, this program is authorized through December 31, 2019.

The Department of Labor's eligibility guidelines define the qualifications of each target group.

Cost Recovery

There are three components to the effective payroll:

  • 52 – 65 weeks of work at a reduced wage
  • The WOTC of $9,600
  • The four-week internship provided at no cost to you

Effective Payroll

It takes $24,000 in wages to generate the maximum WOTC. That total does not change, only the amount of time involved. This means that the wage on paper can vary from about $11.54/hr for a years’ time to the minimum wage of $9.25/hr for 1.25 years’ time. The employer’s share of taxes adds another $2,856 to the cost of payroll, for a total of $26, 856.

Next, that cost is increased by the loss of external tuition revenue and the cost of my tuition. The new total of $36,856 is then reduced by the tuition paid back through my wages (-$5,000) and the WOTC (-$9,600). That brings the effective payroll outlay down $4,600 to $22,256.

Finally, there is a four-week internship involved at no cost to you. For a pay rate of $9.25, this reduces the effective payroll $1,480 to $20,778. For a pay rate of $11.54, the effective payroll is reduced by $1,846 to $20,409.

Effective Hourly Wage (including Employer Tax Portions)

The effective pay rate is dependent on the number of hours worked over a given period of time. For the samples, I used 52-week and 65-week time periods. It’s important to note that the total amount paid does not change, just the period of time. Also, the pay rate listed is not what I would earn, but what you would pay including the employer portion of payroll taxes.

The effective total payroll is obtained by the following:

Describe a Specific Time You Worked on a Close-Kint Team

How did you contribute to the success or failure of the project?

I took a contract to program CNC equipment for a very small startup. The work began with a courier coming to my home almost every morning with a handmade model of the product under development. My job was to measure it within .001”, turn it into a 3D model, then produce a program that would be sent to a machinist to cut them.

Immediately I had trouble reproducing the models, and had several calls with the model maker. I tried to get him to understand that I couldn’t built his prototypes, but to no avail. I just couldn’t understand his unwillingness to fix the problems, as they were clear and easy to understand. For example, ever try to cut a square inside corner with a round tool? Ain’t gonna happen! Finally, he angrily said, “You use a computer; you’re supposed to be able to do things better than what I can do!”, and I began to realize that the conflict was based in fear. This professional manual model maker was not going to be replaced by a stupid computer!

I arranged to go over to his house one afternoon to discuss how to move forward. As he gave me a tour, I realized that he was truly an artist, with examples of beautiful consumer product development he had done. Much of it was on products that most people would have used or at least seen. He took the position, correctly, that product development is more art than science, and that computers were not art.

Just as his world was initially a mystery to me, mine was to him as well. So, I packed up my hundred pounds of computer hardware and took it to his house. I walked through the process, and let him see what was required. It was certainly more complex than he assumed, and he began to see why I was saying there were problems. Soon, we began including the machinist as well, who was able to give me feedback on the speed of my programs and tips on how to approach holding the product during machining.

I made sure to praise their work, and told them often that I couldn’t do their job. I created on-screen representations of actual tools so they could see in advance how they would interact with the design. Very quickly, we began to meet at each other’s homes, or at the company’s office, and design as a team. With my computer modeling skills, the model maker’s design experience and the machinist’s real-world feedback, we were able to keep pace with a very demanding schedule for a first-of-its-kind product.

How did you contribute to the success or failure of the project?

My contributions to the success of the product were

  • my willingness to respect the skill sets of others
  • praising the work of others to give them confidence about their work
  • teaching others about my work process so we could interface more effectively
  • diligently seeking to modify my processes so others could be more efficient

How did other people on the team contribute to the success or failure?

The model maker’s contributions were

  • a willingness to learn about what was to him a very threatening technology
  • a willingness to pass on years of product design wisdom to make my work easier
  • flexibility in work location and timing

The machinist’s contributions were

  • to provide feedback on manufacturing processes
  • education on rapid prototyping tools to speed production

What were your strengths as a group member?

My strengths were mostly technical at the time, but one of the biggest soft strengths was the desire to work together with other experts rather than toss my work over the wall to the next person. Next, I had a strong desire to create the supporting graphics, like exact measurements of tools, to help us find design defects before manufacturing. Finally, I built the project files as a set rather than a series of individual projects. This allowed me to place each layer together, which exposed clearance problems and allowed for rendering of each part, or all, of a product onscreen before manufacturing. While these things are common now, back then this type of creative work was rare. All of these things show my desire to make processes more efficient and products better than the competition.

What were your weaknesses?

  • a tendency to get frustrated about last minute changes to the design by outside influences (the deadlines were very tight, and we worked 6 – 7 days a week, so a seemingly minor change caused havoc on our production schedule)
  • a failure to ask about the bigger picture which made me less supportive of project changes
  • working too long, which led to times when I would miss a chance to work with others as a team because I was sleeping

How did you deal with other group members you didn't get along well with?

There was only one person I didn’t get along with, and the dislike was based in his unwillingness to work with the rest of us. We added a night machinist, and he would do things that would result in models being out of tolerance. He would then say it was my fault for giving him a bad program, even though the day shift machinist produced good parts with the same program. For a while, I tried to use the same techniques I used with the others to gain his trust, but he wasn’t interested in working together with anyone, thus the night shift job. Eventually, we just left him alone until he was fired for producing too many bad parts after the day machinist presented some stats on scrap to the owner.

About this same time, I learned a great lesson from John W. Thompson when he was the VP of Consumer Products at IBM. I ran into him at an airport, and he was kind enough to give me 45 minutes of his time. We discussed my dissatisfaction with IBM’s marketing, and he explained why they were making the decisions they were making. I had an epiphany about this subject. I had assumed that they were a bunch of idiots, but I learned that they had solid reasons for making the decisions they made. The light bulb went on that very few people make truly stupid decisions. If I see someone making such a move, chances are that I don’t have the same set of information as the other person. By respecting them up front, I get the opportunity to hear why those “stupid” decisions were being made so I understand them, and gain the opportunity to have input into them if I have relevant information to add.
